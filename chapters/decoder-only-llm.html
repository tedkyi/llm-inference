2  The Decoder-Only Transformer

This chapter covers the architecture of the basic decoder-only transformer architecture used by most LLMs such as ChatGPT and Llama. We will walk through in enough detail to be able to trace a token through the full model, seeing the parameter and activation tensor dimensions. If you’re already comfortable with multi-head self-attention, the KV cache, and gated MLPs, you can skim through this material and move ahead to the next chapter. We recommend that you examine Tip 2.1 to understand the conventions used in data flow diagrams before moving ahead to the rest of the book.

We’ll use concrete numbers from the Llama family of models throughout. Not because Llama is special, but because its architecture is representative of modern LLMs and its parameters are public. When we say “a 70B model,” we mean something with roughly Llama 3 70B’s dimensions: 80 layers, a model dimension of 8192, 64 attention heads, and a vocabulary of 128,000 tokens (Grattafiori et al. 2024).

2.1 From Encoder-Decoder to Decoder-Only

The original transformer architecture introduced in “Attention Is All You Need” (Vaswani et al. 2017) has two distinct halves: an encoder that processes the input sequence and a decoder that generates the output sequence. The encoder builds a rich representation of the input through layers of self-attention. The decoder then attends to that representation through cross-attention layers while also attending to its own previously generated tokens through masked self-attention.

This encoder-decoder design, shown in Figure 2.1 was built for sequence-to-sequence tasks like machine translation, where you have a clear separation between input (such as a sentence in French) and output (its English translation). The encoder reads the full input and builds a bidirectional representation – each token can attend to every other token, past and future. The decoder then generates the output one token at a time, attending both to the encoder’s representation and to its own partial output.

The decoder-only transformer, used by GPT-2 (Radford et al. 2019), LLaMA (Touvron, Lavril, et al. 2023), and virtually all modern large language models in production today, takes this architecture and strips it down. The entire encoder is removed. The cross-attention layers in the decoder are removed. What’s left is a single stack of transformer blocks, each containing masked self-attention and a feed-forward network, preceded by a token embedding layer and followed by a linear language model head.

Researchers also found that training larger models was more stable when the normalization happened before each attention and FFN block, instead of after. This pre-norm formulation is shown in Figure 2.2. On the left is a trimmed down version of the original transformer architecture. On the right is an equivalent diagram where the attention and FFN layers have been placed to the side, making the residual connection path, known as the residual stream more obvious. Throughout this book, we will use this straight through arrangement for clearer intuition.

Why did this simpler architecture win? Three reasons stand out. First, simplicity: one stack of identical blocks is easier to implement, optimize, and scale than two interleaved stacks. Second, favorable scaling behavior: empirically, decoder-only models have shown strong performance scaling with increased parameters and data, as demonstrated by GPT-3, PaLM (Chowdhery et al. 2022), and the Llama series. Third, a unified pretraining objective: next-token prediction works on any text, requiring no paired input-output data. You can perform self-supervised training on the entire internet without needing aligned corpora or labeled data. Another key benefit is that the decoder-only model is faster to train than the original encoder-decoder transformer.

Layer repetition and weight sharing

Two aspects of the architecture are worth making very explicit, because they matter for understanding inference costs.

First, the “N×” in the architecture diagram means that the same block structure is repeated N times. Note that each layer has the same structure, shape, and size, but each layer has its own independent set of weights. Layer 0’s attention weights are completely separate from Layer 1’s. A 70B model with 80 layers has 80 distinct sets of attention and feed-forward weights. During inference, the model must read each layer’s weights from memory in sequence – there is no reuse across layers. A diagram making the N copies explicit is shown in Figure 2.3. This is what a decoder-only LLM really looks like, and if you review the PyTorch code for one, you will see this exact structure. We will mention that the input to the first layers is a vector of floating point numbers of length \(d_{model}\), which will will abbreviate D.

Second, the architecture is applied identically across every position in the sequence, sharing all weights. When we process a sequence of S tokens, the same weight matrices are applied to every token position. Figure 2.4 shows an intuition of the model being copied repeatedly for each token position.

This idea of multiple copies of our model is a good mental model to understand data flow across token positions, however it is also important to understand that this is not how model inference happens. Rather than making copies of the model, we simply take our vertical stack model and change the input from a 1D vector of shape (D) to a 2D tensor of shape (S, D).

Note

For efficiency reasons, almost all deep learning training and inference happens in batches. This is true as well for LLM inference, and we will discuss batching in Section 5.1. For the sake of brevity, especially in diagrams, we will discuss non-batched data flow and omit the explicit mention of the batch dimension. For LLMs, the full shape of the input to the first layer is (B, S, D), which we abbreviate to (S, D) for non-batched inputs. Wherever tensor shapes are mentioned without a leading batch dimension, it is appropriate to think about batched processing adding a single dimension to the beginning of the shape. We hope this convention of using non-batched shapes does not cause any confusion.

2.2 Forward Pass Data Flow

To better understand how inference works, we will examine what happens when we send data through a forward pass of the model. We will start at the big picture and work our way down into the individual components. Let’s trace input token IDs from input to output logits, annotating every tensor shape along the way.

Tip 2.1

Before diving into the data flow, here are a few conventions used in the data flow diagrams.

In this example data flow diagram, the tensors are represented by rectangles, and the operand a rounded rectangle.

Dimensions and edge color: The data tensor’s shape is shown next to it, on the right side unless there isn’t room. This first tensor on the bottom has shape (S, D), where S is the row dimension and D is the column dimension. Note that the left and right edges are colored red, and every tensor with a dimension that is length S will have that dimension colored red. Similarly, the top and bottom edges are colored blue because the column dimension is D, and all other dimensions length D will be the same color.

Fill color: The fill color of the tensor is gray. Tensors holding similar semantic information will have similar fill colors. Weights are fixed model tensors, and they always have the same purple fill color.

3D tensors: The tensor at the top of the diagram is 3D with shape (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)). Because it is 3D, it is shown with a “stacked” rectangle shape. The leading dimension, H, is the stacked dimension. The third dimsion’s purple edge color corresponds to this dimension.

Views: The middle tensor to the top tensor are connected by a dashed line. This indicates that there is a change of view, but no actual operation is performed.

The input

The input to our model is a list of integer token IDs. It’s shape is (S), where S is the sequence length (number of tokens). As a reminder, in this book we are using the simpler notation of non-batched processing. For batched processing, the input shape would be (B, S). The value of S varies. S starts as the length of the prompt, and it increases as generated tokens are appended. It is reasonable that S will be less than D at the beginning of generation, but S will be greater than D by the end of generating a long response. We will call this input tensor \(X\).

The full model

The complete forward pass stacks N decoder blocks between the embedding layer and the language model head.

Positional encoding

One detail we’ve glossed over: how does the model know the order of tokens? The self-attention computation is permutation-equivariant – if you shuffle the input tokens, the output gets shuffled the same way (ignoring the causal mask). Position information must be explicitly injected.

Two approaches are common:

Learned absolute embeddings (GPT-2): a learnable embedding table of shape (max_seq_len, D) is added to the token embeddings. Position 0 gets one learned vector, position 1 gets another, and so on. Simple, but the model can’t generalize to positions longer than its training context.

Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al. 2021): instead of adding position information to the input, RoPE applies a position-dependent rotation to the query and key vectors inside the attention computation. The rotation is defined such that the dot product between a query at position \(i\) and a key at position \(j\) depends only on the relative distance \(i - j\). This is what most modern LLMs use, including Llama and its derivatives. RoPE enables better length generalization and is applied after the Q and K projections but before the attention score computation.

For inference purposes, the distinction matters mainly for two reasons. First, RoPE’s relative position encoding is what enables context length extension techniques. Second, RoPE is applied inside the attention block at a specific point in the computation, which matters when implementing custom attention kernels.

Weight tying

Some architectures share weights between the token embedding layer, whose shape is (V, D) and the LM head, whose shape is (D, V), which is the transpose of the embedding layer. This is called weight tying. When the vocabulary is large, these matrices are significant – for V = 128,000 and D = 8192, each matrix has about 1B parameters. Tying them saves 1B parameters and ensures that the model’s input and output representations are in the same space.

Weight tying matters for inference because it affects the parameter count and, consequently, the quantization analysis in Section 4.1. If the embedding and LM head share weights, quantizing one affects the other.

A single decoder block

Drilling into each layer in the model, each decoder block in a modern LLM uses the pre-norm formulation:

\[\begin{aligned} \hat{X} &= X + \text{SelfAttention}(\text{LayerNorm}(X)) \\ y &= \hat{X} + \text{FFN}(\text{LayerNorm}(\hat{X})) \end{aligned}\]

LayerNorm (Ba et al. 2016) is applied before each sub-layer (self-attention and FFN), and the sub-layer’s output is added to its input via a residual connection. This is the pre-norm formulation, used by GPT-2 (Radford et al. 2019) and all modern LLMs. The original transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017) used post-norm (LayerNorm after the residual add), but pre-norm is now standard because it provides better training stability at scale.

The residual connections are critical for training deep networks – they allow gradients to flow through the network without vanishing. For inference, they’re simple addition operations that don’t add meaningful compute cost.

2.3 Self-Attention Mechanics

Self-attention is the mechanism that lets each token’s representation incorporate information from other tokens in the sequence. This is what makes a transformer more than just a position-independent feature extractor – it’s what lets the model understand that “bank” means something different in “river bank” versus “bank account.” We’ll walk through the computation step by step, tracking tensor shapes throughout. We will dissect the components and data flow of the attention layer in this section. If the reader is unfamiliar with attention, we recommend some of the resources in the Further Reading at the end of this chapter.

The input

The input to a self-attention layer is a tensor of shape (S, D), where S is the sequence length (number of tokens), and D is the model dimension (the width of each token’s representation vector). As a reminder, in this book we are using the simpler notation of non-batched processing. For batched processing, the input shape would be (B, S, D). For Llama 70B, D = 8192.

Q, K, V projections

The first step of self-attention is to project the input into three separate representations: queries (Q), keys (K), and values (V). Each projection is a learned linear transformation:

\[Q = X W_Q, \quad K = X W_K, \quad V = X W_V\]

where \(X\) has shape (S, D) and \(W_Q\), \(W_K\), \(W_V\) each have shape (D, D). The outputs Q, K, and V all have shape (S, D).

The intuition is roughly this: the query represents “what this token is looking for,” the key represents “what this token offers to other tokens,” and the value represents “the information this token will contribute when attended to.” These are loose analogies, but they help build intuition for why we need three separate projections rather than just one or two.

Multi-head attention and the reshape

Rather than computing a single attention function over the full D-dimensional space, we split the computation into H parallel heads, each operating on a subspace of dimension \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K} = \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} / \textcolor{#A855F7}{H}\). For Llama 70B, H = 64 heads and \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\) = 128.

After the linear projections, we reshape Q, K, and V from (S, D) to (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)). Mathematically, this is just reshaping the last dimension – breaking D into H groups of \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\) – and transposing so the head dimension comes before the sequence dimension. No additional computation is required.

Why multiple heads? A single attention head computes a single attention pattern – one set of weights determining how much each position attends to every other position. Multiple heads allow the model to attend to different aspects of the input simultaneously. One head might focus on syntactic relationships, another on semantic similarity, and another on positional proximity. In practice, learned attention patterns are more complex than these examples, but the principle holds: multiple heads provide multiple “channels” for cross-position communication.

Attention score computation

With Q and K reshaped to (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)), we compute the raw attention scores by taking the dot product of queries with keys:

\[\text{scores} = \frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_K}}\]

The matrix multiplication \(Q K^T\) produces a tensor of shape (H, S, S) – for each head in each batch element, we get a matrix shape (S, S) where entry (i, j) is the raw attention score between position i and position j. The division by \(\sqrt{d_K}\) prevents the dot products from growing too large as the dimension increases, which would push softmax into regions with vanishing gradients.

This (H, S, S) attention matrix is the computational signature of standard self-attention. Its size is quadratic in sequence length – for a 4096-token prompt, each head produces a 4096×4096 matrix. This quadratic scaling is why long-context inference is expensive and why techniques like FlashAttention (Dao et al. 2022) are so important. FlashAttention computes the exact same result but avoids materializing the full S×S matrix in HBM by tiling the computation to fit in on-chip SRAM.

The causal mask

In a decoder-only model used for autoregressive generation, each token should only attend to itself and to tokens that came before it – never to future tokens. This is enforced by a causal mask: an upper-triangular matrix of \(-\infty\) values added to the attention scores before softmax.

After adding the mask, we apply softmax along the key dimension (the last dimension). The \(-\infty\) values become zero after softmax, ensuring each position’s attention weights sum to 1 over only the allowed (past and current) positions.

Weighted sum and output projection

The softmax output gives us the attention weights: a (H, S, S) tensor where each row sums to 1 (or less, after masking). We use these weights to compute a weighted sum of the value vectors:

\[\text{output} = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_K}} + \text{mask}\right) V\]

The attention weights have shape (H, S, S) and V has shape (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)), so the result has shape (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)). We then reshape this back from (H, S, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)) to (S, D) by concatenating the H heads, and apply a final output projection \(W_O\) of shape (D, D):

\[\text{MultiHead}(X) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_H) \cdot W_O\]

The full self-attention block transforms (S, D) to (S, D) – the shape is preserved.

Parameter count

For standard multi-head attention with D = 8192, the parameter count per layer is:

  • \(W_Q\): \(\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\) = 67.1M
  • \(W_K\): \(\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\) = 67.1M
  • \(W_V\): \(\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\) = 67.1M
  • \(W_O\): \(\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\) = 67.1M
  • Total: \(4\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}^2 \approx\) 268M parameters per attention layer

With 80 layers, the attention parameters alone account for about 21.5B of Llama 70B’s parameter budget. The rest is in the feed-forward layers, which we’ll cover in Section 2.5.

Note

Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) and friends. Modern models often use fewer key-value heads than query heads. Llama 3 70B uses GQA (Ainslie et al. 2023) with 64 query heads but only 8 KV heads, reducing \(W_K\) and \(W_V\) from (D, D) to (D, \(8 \times \textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\)) = (8192, 1024). This saves parameters and, more importantly, reduces the KV cache size by 8×. Multi-Query Attention (MQA) (Shazeer 2019) takes this further with a single KV head. Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) (Liu et al. 2024) compresses the KV cache through learned low-rank projections. These variants are covered in Section 4.4.

A note on alternatives

Standard multi-head attention dominates deployed LLMs today, but it’s not the only game in town. State-space models like Mamba (Gu and Dao 2023) and linear attention variants replace the (H, S, S) attention matrix with a fixed-size recurrent state. Instead of caching all past keys and values (which grows linearly with sequence length), they maintain a constant-size state that gets updated at each step. This eliminates the KV cache entirely, giving a fundamentally different inference profile – constant memory per step instead of linearly growing memory.

These architectures are out of scope for this book. We focus on standard multi-head attention because it remains dominant in the models that practitioners actually deploy at scale. But it’s worth knowing the alternative exists, especially since the KV cache is a major source of inference complexity.

2.4 The KV Cache — An Inference Perspective

The KV cache is one of the most important concepts in LLM inference. It’s a simple idea with far-reaching consequences for memory management, scheduling, and system design. This section explains why the cache exists, what it stores, and how much memory it consumes.

Training vs. inference

During training, the full sequence is available upfront. The model processes all S tokens at once, computing Q, K, and V for every position simultaneously. The causal mask ensures that position \(i\) only attends to positions \(0, 1, \ldots, i\), but all positions are computed in parallel. There’s no need to cache anything – the K and V tensors for all positions are computed in one pass and used immediately.

During inference, tokens are generated one at a time. At decode step \(t\), the model generates token \(t\) based on all previous tokens \(0, 1, \ldots, t-1\). The new token’s query needs to attend to the keys and values of all previous positions. This creates a choice:

The naive approach: at each step, feed all \(t\) tokens through the model from scratch. This recomputes K and V for positions \(0\) through \(t-1\), even though they haven’t changed since last time. Over S decode steps, the total computation is \(O(\textcolor{#DC2626}{S}^{2})\) – we recompute increasingly long sequences for each new token. This is wasteful.

The KV cache approach: store the K and V tensors from all previous positions. At each decode step, compute K and V for only the new token, append them to the cache, and use the full cached K and V for the attention computation. This reduces the redundant recomputation to \(O(\textcolor{#DC2626}{S} )\) total across all decode steps.

Memory consumption

The KV cache stores keys and values for every layer, every KV head, every cached position, and every element of the head dimension. The total memory for a single request is:

\[\text{KV cache memory} = 2 \times \textcolor{#8B4513}{L} \times \textcolor{#C084FC}{H_{\text{KV}}} \times \textcolor{#DC2626}{S} \times \textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K} \times b\]

where L is the number of layers, \(\textcolor{#C084FC}{H_{\text{KV}}}\) is the number of key-value heads, S is the current sequence length, \(\textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K}\) is the head dimension, and \(b\) is the number of bytes per element (2 for FP16/BF16, 1 for FP8/INT8).

For Llama 3 70B with GQA (8 KV heads), FP16 precision, and a 4096-token sequence:

\[2 \times 80 \times 8 \times 4096 \times 128 \times 2 \approx 1.34 \text{ GB}\]

That’s 1.34 GB for a single request. If you’re serving 40 concurrent requests, the KV cache alone consumes over 50 GB – on an 80 GB GPU that also needs to hold the model weights. This is why KV cache memory management is such a central concern in LLM serving systems.

The cache grows by a fixed amount at each decode step: \(2 \times \textcolor{#8B4513}{L} \times \textcolor{#C084FC}{H_{\text{kv}}} \times \textcolor{#2563EB}{d_K} \times b\) bytes per token. For the Llama 70B numbers above, that’s about 328 KB per token per request. Over a 4096-token generation, the cache grows from zero to 1.34 GB.

Note

Forward references. The KV cache drives many of the optimization techniques covered in the main chapters. Section 4.4 covers architectural techniques that reduce the cache size: MQA, GQA, and MLA reduce the number of KV heads; Cross-Layer Attention shares caches across layers. Section 6.3 covers runtime techniques for managing the cache: PagedAttention (Kwon et al. 2023) eliminates fragmentation, KV cache quantization reduces the bytes per element, and eviction policies drop less-important cache entries under memory pressure.

2.5 The Feed-Forward Network

Every transformer layer contains two main sub-layers: self-attention and a feed-forward network (FFN), also called the MLP block. While self-attention handles cross-position communication, the FFN operates on each position independently, transforming the representation at each position through nonlinear learned mappings. The FFN is where the majority of a model’s parameters live.

Standard MLP

The original transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017) uses a two-layer MLP with a ReLU activation:

\[\text{FFN}(x) = \text{ReLU}(x W_1 + b_1) W_2 + b_2\]

The input \(X\) has shape (S, D). The first linear layer “up-projects” from D to an intermediate dimension F, originally F = 4D. The activation function is applied element-wise. Then the second linear layer “down-projects” back from F to D.

Gated MLP (SwiGLU)

Modern LLMs like Llama and PaLM don’t use the standard MLP. They use a gated variant called SwiGLU (Shazeer 2020), which adds a third linear layer and uses a gating mechanism:

\[\text{FFN}_{\text{SwiGLU}}(x) = (\text{SiLU}(x W_{\text{gate}}) \odot x W_{\text{up}}) W_{\text{down}}\]

where \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication and SiLU (also called Swish) is the activation function \(\text{SiLU}(x) = x \cdot \sigma(x)\).

The gate and up projections run in parallel, both mapping from D to an intermediate dimension F. Their outputs are combined by element-wise multiplication – the gate “selects” which features from the up-projection pass through. The result is then down-projected back to D.

Why go from two weight matrices to three? The gating mechanism has been shown empirically to improve model quality for a given parameter budget (Shazeer 2020). The intermediate dimension F is adjusted downward (from \(4\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\) to roughly \(\frac{8}{3}\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}\)) to keep the total parameter count approximately equal: \(3 \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times \frac{8}{3}\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} = 8\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}^2\), the same as the standard \(2 \times \textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} \times 4\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D} = 8\textcolor{#1E40AF}{D}^2\).

Mixture of Experts (MoE)

Standard dense models apply the same MLP to every token. Mixture of Experts (MoE) architectures replace the single MLP with \(E\) expert MLPs and a lightweight router that selects which experts to use for each token.

The router is a simple learned linear layer of shape (D, E) that produces a score for each expert. For each token, the router selects the top-\(k\) experts (typically \(k\) = 2), and only those experts process the token. The outputs of the selected experts are combined using the router’s softmax weights.

The key trade-off of MoE for inference is clear: memory footprint is large (all expert weights must reside in memory) but per-token compute is modest (only \(k\) of \(E\) experts are active). Mixtral 8×7B (Jiang et al. 2024) has roughly 47B total parameters but only ~13B active per token. This means the model takes up as much GPU memory as a 47B dense model but computes each token about as fast as a 13B dense model.

For multi-GPU serving, MoE models naturally lend themselves to expert parallelism – placing different experts on different GPUs. But this introduces all-to-all communication as tokens are routed to the GPU holding their selected expert. Load balancing across experts also matters: if the router sends most tokens to the same few experts, some GPUs do much more work than others. We’ll revisit expert parallelism in Chapter 7.

Why the FFN matters for inference

The MLP layers typically account for roughly two-thirds of a model’s parameters. For Llama 70B, each layer’s SwiGLU MLP has about 704M parameters versus about 268M for attention, a ratio of roughly 2.6:1. Across 80 layers, that’s about 56B parameters in MLPs versus 21B in attention.

During decode, every active parameter is read from HBM once per token (at batch size 1). The MLP weights are the single largest contributor to the memory bandwidth demand of each decode step. This is why quantization (Section 4.1) has such an outsized effect: compressing the MLP weights from FP16 to INT4 roughly halves the memory bandwidth needed per decode step, directly increasing the achievable tokens-per-second.

Prefill vs. decode through the full model

With the full architecture in view, we can be precise about the difference between prefill and decode:

Prefill processes S tokens simultaneously. Every tensor has its full S dimension. The Q×K^T computation produces (H, S, S) attention matrices – large matrix-matrix multiplications that are compute-bound. The KV cache is written at every layer (storing K and V for all S positions) but not read. The output is logits of shape (S, V), though we typically only use the last position’s logits to generate the first output token.

Decode processes 1 token at a time. The input is (1, D). The Q×K^T computation produces (H, 1, \(S_{\text{cache}}\)) – a matrix-vector multiplication where \(S_{\text{cache}}\) is the number of tokens currently in the cache. This is memory-bandwidth-bound because the full weight matrices and the full KV cache must be read for just one token’s worth of output. The KV cache is both read (to retrieve all past keys and values) and written (to append the new token’s K and V).

This asymmetry is the fundamental challenge of LLM inference and the motivation for most of the techniques covered in the main chapters of this book.

2.6 Further Reading

The original transformer paper (Vaswani et al. 2017) is the essential starting point. Even though the decoder-only variant drops half the architecture, the core ideas – scaled dot-product attention, multi-head attention, positional encoding, and the feed-forward network – are all introduced there.

For intuitive explanations of the transformer architecture:

  • Peter Bloem’s “Transformers from Scratch” (Bloem 2019) builds the architecture from first principles with clear diagrams.
  • Lilian Weng’s “The Transformer Family Version 2.0” (Weng 2023) surveys transformer variants including the attention alternatives mentioned in Section 2.3.
  • Andrej Karpathy’s “Let’s build GPT: from scratch, in code” (Karpathy 2023) walks through a complete implementation in about two hours.

For the specific architectural choices used in modern LLMs, the Llama papers (Touvron, Lavril, et al. 2023; Touvron, Martin, et al. 2023; Grattafiori et al. 2024) are a good reference because they clearly document the model dimensions and design decisions (SwiGLU, RoPE, GQA). The PaLM paper (Chowdhery et al. 2022) provides a thorough analysis of scaling behavior that motivates the decoder-only design.
